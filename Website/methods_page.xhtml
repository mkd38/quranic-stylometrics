Digital Humanities, Explained

Digital humanities is a field concerned with the use of computational tools to enhance the study of humanist disciplines.

Processing

The Qur'an is organized by chapters and verses, henceforth surahs and ayahs, respectively. Due to the constraints of this project, the team members selected three surahs for analysis. These surahs were selected subjectively based on cultural significance and richness of the text. Our were texts sourced from the Perseus Digital Library under Creative Commons licensing. Using XML, the texts were annotated to tag instances of eleven different stylistic devices.

Our Devices

  • Amplification: This device refers to language which is used to glorify God. Example: "Most Merciful, All Knowing..."
  • Simile: A comparison of one or more thing using like or as. Example: "His light is like a niche in which there is a lamp, the lamp is in a crystal, the crystal is like a shining star..."
  • Metaphor: A comparison of one or more thing. Example: "We have certainly cast veils over their hearts—leaving them unable to comprehend this ˹Quran˺—and deafness in their ears."
  • Imagery: Figurative language that creates a mental image. Example: "Let them then taste this: boiling water and ˹oozing˺ pus..."
  • Repetition: The action of repeating something that has already been said or written. (if you find instances of repetition that are not distinctly parallelism/anaphora)
  • Parallelism: The use of similar words, clauses, phrases, sentence structure, or other grammatical elements to emphasize similar ideas in a sentence. Example: "Let the man of wealth provide according to his means. As for the one with limited resources, let him provide according to whatever Allah has given him."   • Allusion: A symbolical reference is called allusion. Allusion is an indirect reference in a discourse by passing mention or quotation, to something generally familiar. Example: "May the hands of Abu Lahab perish, and he ˹himself˺ perish!"
